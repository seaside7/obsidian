
At a high level, the Katingan Mentaya project is a nature-based carbon project that generates carbon credits by protecting forests and preventing emissions.

The process starts with defining a baseline scenario. This means estimating what would happen if the forest was not protected—for example, deforestation that would release a certain amount of CO₂ into the atmosphere.

Next, the project implements conservation activities, such as protecting peatland forests and preventing land conversion. By doing this, the project avoids the emissions that would have occurred under the baseline scenario.

The avoided emissions are then measured using established methodologies. This includes calculating the difference between the baseline emissions and the actual emissions after protection. The result is the total amount of CO₂ avoided.

After that, the data goes through a strict verification process by independent third-party auditors under standards like VCS (Verified Carbon Standard). They validate whether the methodology, calculations, and field data are accurate and credible.

Once verified, the avoided emissions are converted into carbon credits, where each credit represents one ton of CO₂. These credits are issued and recorded in a registry system, each with a unique identifier to prevent double counting.

Companies such as Indocement can then purchase these credits. When they do, they are not transferring their emissions to the project. Instead, they are paying for emission reductions that have already been achieved and verified by the project.

Finally, the buyer can use these credits to offset their emissions for reporting, ESG commitments, or compliance purposes, while the project continues ongoing monitoring and verification to maintain credibility over time.




The whole system is essentially turning real-world environmental protection into a digital, tradable asset — but that asset only has value if the underlying data is trusted.”
